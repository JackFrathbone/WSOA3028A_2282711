Blog 8: On the Digital Divide

The internet has become more and more accessible and widely available with each year, as globally prices drop and devices like smartphones become even more commonplace. But no matter how affordable and universal the internet becomes, it will always be a reflection of humanity across the world, including the inequality between people and places. The idea of the digital divide is that real world inequality is now reflected online, as with so many key services and important information being accessible on the internet, and new services like news and internet shopping, people who do not have access to the internet are becoming increasingly disadvantaged. Also part of this gap between those with access and those without, is that those without internet don’t have any say in the content online so historically privileged peoples take up a greater share of the internet and have a greater influence on its content.

Mark Graham in the essay Internet Geographies: Data Shadows and Digital Divisions of Labour examines how historical and real world inequalities are reflected in online spaces (Graham, 2014). One important aspect, that is very useful from an african perspective, is how those groups privileged with more and longer term internet access, take up a larger part of the internet, their stories and ideas feature much more and their influence is stronger. This means that overall african voices are much rarer online than those from the United States and Europe, who tend to culturally dominate big internet spaces like Youtube and Twitter, which means stories and ideas from many parts of the world are harder to find and share, and the overall ‘culture’ of the internet becomes more western centric.

Graham shows how the historical inequalities between regions that affect other media, also affects the internet, and the importance of building a better internet that is more accessible and open to all peoples, an internet that actively combats digital divides that exist, because this allows more perspectives and voices to flourish online. Robinson et al also point out that “digital inequalities continue to combine with race, class, gender, and other offline axes of inequality” (Robinson et al, 2015: 2) which shows how any fight against global inequality both affects the internet, but also is affected by the internet, which serves as a platform for all people who have access to it.

Inequality has consequences both in reality and digitally, and if things are left as they are the internet will continue to be dominated by privileged voices that sideline and marginalise the majority of people in the world who come from countries with less access. As long as digital inequality exists then the internet will function as a segregated space that provides access for only a section of humanity.

With all this in mind, it raises the importance of thinking about digital divides when designing online spaces, and how we can continue to build an internet that combats marginalisation and welcomes more people. While designers may not be able to put more people online, they can make sure those with less access get a bigger share in terms of space to tell stories and share information, creating a more diverse and representative internet for all of humanity, breaking the barriers that exist in the real world.

References:

Graham, M. (2014) Internet Geographies: Data Shadows and Digital Divisions of Labour. Society and the Internet: How Networks of Information and Communication are Changing our Lives, eds M. Graham and W. H. Dutton. Oxford: Oxford University Press. 99-116.

Robinson, L., Cotten, S., Ono H., Quan-Haase A., Mesch, G., Chen W., Schulz J., Hale, T., Stern, M. (2015) Digital inequalities and why they matter. Information, Communication & Society. 18:5, 569-582.
