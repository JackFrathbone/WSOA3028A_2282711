Blog 6: Reflection on Alternative Approaches to Interface Technology

This article written by Steve Gibson back in 2004 is a particularly interesting highlight of how little computer interfaces have really changed in over 17 years, and how the new suggestion of ways we can radically change our interfaces are similar to the ideas from back in the 90’s (Gibson, 2004). In the article Gibson talks firstly about the main limitations present in modern computer interfaces, mainly the mouse and keyboard, which he says are developed along the lines of practicality and ergonomics. But these interfaces don’t break radically in any way from analogue interfaces of the past such as typewriters which keyboards evolved from, and they don't take advantage of the wide array of new technologies that show different and new ways people can interact with computers.

The main content of the articles looks at these new computer interfaces, from hands free tracking and MIDI based controls, to the more strange electrode based controls of the artist Stelarc's cybernetics. Gibson shows these different forms of interface not as an alternative to the keyboard and mouse as a new standard, but instead raises the idea of more specialised and alternative computer interfaces that come with completely different design and artistic philosophies to the standardised and practical keyboard. This is the most interesting and important aspect of the article, because it raises many more questions about how the standardisation of computer interfaces may itself be a limit on digital interaction and creativity, and how the way we interact with a computer or device can completely change the way we use and think about it.

In terms of my own general work, this article makes me think about how common interfaces like keyboards and phone touch screens completely guide but also limit the ways we develop programs and websites, and this both makes it easy to design for an average computer user it also puts strict limitations on how most people interact with computers. By starting to think about alternate interfaces, designers can both realise the limitations of our current interfaces and how in the future we can push design towards new and exciting forms of computer interaction.

For now Gibson also raises the point about how changing the way we interact with something can really change how we relate and understand it. While designing a website usually it is designed to be as easily understandable and navigable with the normal interfaces like mouse, touch screen etc, but by changing the interface or using it differently it can completely change the feel of the website and create certain emotions. For example if a user was forced to navigate a page solely with their keyboard where a mouse would be normal, this may make the user frustrated, but could also be done in such a way to feel unique or new, and force the user to experiment with these alternate controls.

While the alternative interfaces mentioned by Gibson never became widely used or known, the idea of rethinking the general approach to computer interfaces, and the result of using more specialised interfaces, does raise many interesting questions about how the technologies of the future might finally break the standard forms we have been using with computers for the last 30+ years, and the new possibilities this offers for designers.

References:

Gibson, S. (2004). Alternative Approaches to Interface Technology. Perspectives on Multimedia: Communication, Media and Information Technology. London: John Wiley and Sons, pp. 17-39.
